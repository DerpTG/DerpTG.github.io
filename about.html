About

The short version of who I am, how I got here, and what I do when I'm not reconstructing someone else's bad day.

Who I am

I'm a recent Penn State graduate (B.S. Cybersecurity Analytics and Operations, summa cum laude) who got into security through the part most people skip: the aftermath. My academic work centered on digital forensics and incident response — reconstructing a ransomware attack packet by packet, taking malware apart to see what it does before it does it, and writing the playbooks that decide who gets called and when.

Alongside that, my day-to-day work at Stemuli has been the governance side of the house: privacy compliance, control assessments, and the documentation that keeps a live K-12 platform inside the lines of COPPA, FERPA, NIST, and CIS. It's taught me that security is a systems problem, not just a technical one — the best control in the world fails if nobody owns it or the policy behind it is unclear.

What I'm after now is a hands-on analyst seat — SOC, security analyst, or incident response — somewhere with mentorship and room to grow. I'm a coachable new grad, not a finished expert: I ask questions early, chase down the root cause instead of the symptom, and write things down so the next person doesn't have to relearn them. This site is part of that habit — everything on it is real work, documented so you can actually poke at it.

Beyond work

The things that keep me curious when I'm off the clock.

Hands-on

Building computers

I build my own machines from parts, and that first successful boot never gets old. It's the same loop as the rest of my work — plan it, assemble it, and troubleshoot calmly when it doesn't post the first time.

Obsession

Haute horologie

I'm fascinated by mechanical watchmaking — hundreds of tiny parts engineered to keep time with nothing but a wound spring. It's precision and constraint as a craft, which is probably why it appeals to the same part of my brain that likes forensics.

Active

Fitness & biking

Lifting and cycling are how I clear my head. Biking especially — long rides are good thinking time, and they're the counterweight to a job that's mostly done sitting in front of a screen.

Reading

Dystopian fiction

I get completely absorbed in dystopian novels. There's an obvious throughline with security work — both are, at heart, about how systems fail and what people do when the rules stop holding.
